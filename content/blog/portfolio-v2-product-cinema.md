---
title: Rebuilding The Portfolio As Product Cinema
date: "2026-05-02"
excerpt: Why I cut the homepage down, added a cinematic WebGL system, and made the portfolio itself behave like proof instead of a long explanation.
---

The previous portfolio was honest, but it was doing too much explaining.

It had the right ingredients: Sideout, DraftLens, proof, writing, experience, GitHub links, and a clear developer story. The issue was density. Too many sections were trying to repeat the same message in different words.

For V2, I wanted the site to say less and prove more.

## The first decision was subtraction

The homepage now has a much sharper job:

- introduce who I am
- make Sideout and DraftLens feel like flagship systems
- show the connection between product, frontend craft, backend thinking, AI, and commerce
- move recruiters toward work, resume, GitHub, or contact

That meant cutting the older about, experience, writing intro, GitHub shelf, and repeated proof language from the homepage. Those ideas still exist in the site, but they do not all need to fight for first-screen attention.

The strongest homepage version is shorter because the work has to carry more of the weight.

## The visual system had to mean something

I did not want a decorative 3D background that looked expensive but said nothing.

The new visual direction uses a cosmic and computational language because it maps to the portfolio story:

- Sideout and DraftLens orbit the same product judgment
- the Product Universe connects AI, commerce, data, backend, and outcomes
- the black-hole field bends light at the top of the page
- scrolling resolves that physics into a neural/circuit graph
- compiler glyphs and spectral code sit behind the product surfaces

The goal is not realism. It is a feeling: ambitious systems, compressed signal, and a portfolio that behaves like a product artifact instead of a resume stretched into a website.

## The technical constraint mattered

WebGL is powerful, but it can also make a portfolio fragile if it becomes the only way to understand the page.

So the V2 architecture keeps the spectacle in client-only islands and keeps critical content in normal HTML. The canvas can fail and the site still works. Reduced motion gets a static path. Links, resume actions, case-study summaries, and writing remain readable without needing the visual layer.

That is the balance I wanted: cinematic, but not brittle.

## What changed about the story

The portfolio now has a clearer point of view.

It is not "here are some projects." It is "here are two flagship systems, here is the product range they prove, and here is the level of frontend craft I want to be judged by."

That feels closer to the kind of engineer I am trying to become: someone who can build the product, shape the interface, explain the tradeoffs, and make the whole thing feel worth taking seriously.
