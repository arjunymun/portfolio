Sideout became stronger when I stopped thinking about it as "a booking app" and started treating it like the operating system around a real venue.

The inspiration was specific: a family-run pickleball academy in Dehradun. That mattered because the product could not hide behind generic sports-tech language. It needed to feel like something an actual court operator could understand, maintain, and use to bring players back.

## The product had to model the business

A venue is not just a calendar. It has guests, members, court demand, repeat customers, offline habits, credits, offers, and operator judgment.

That changed the scope of Sideout. The product needed a public surface that could sell the venue, a customer surface that could make value visible, and an operator surface that could keep daily work under control.

The core system became:

- public venue positioning
- guest and customer booking flows
- memberships, packs, credits, and wallet value
- booking approvals and schedule control
- customer notes, inactivity signals, and retention actions
- India-first pricing and venue language

That range is the reason Sideout matters in the portfolio. It proves I can build outside the AI-demo lane and still make the product feel coherent.

## The important design decision was repeat play

The easiest version of Sideout would have been a clean court scheduler. That would have looked fine, but it would not have said much.

The better question was: what would make a player come back?

That question pushed the design toward value systems instead of only transactions. Credits, packs, memberships, offers, and wallet balances became part of the interface because they explain why a customer would keep booking through the product instead of treating it as a one-time checkout.

For the operator, the same idea shows up differently. The dashboard is not just "admin controls." It is a place to see requests, occupancy, revenue context, customer history, and follow-up opportunities.

## Why it belongs beside DraftLens

DraftLens proves that I can make an AI workflow feel structured and trustworthy. Sideout proves a different kind of judgment: commercial software, operational logic, and multi-surface product design.

Together they make the portfolio more believable.

One project is about academic feedback, citations, saved reports, and careful AI framing. The other is about bookings, money, customer value, and venue operations. The overlap is the craft: typed systems, readable interfaces, and product stories that hold up under review.

That is what I wanted Sideout to do. Not just add another card to the portfolio, but widen the proof.
