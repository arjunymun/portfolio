---
title: DraftLens Needed To Read Like A Product, Not Just An AI Demo
date: "2026-03-31"
excerpt: What changed when I stopped treating DraftLens like a one-off prototype and started shaping it like a student-facing product.
---

DraftLens started as a practical experiment: could I build a tool that gave students useful essay feedback without forcing them through a confusing or overly academic workflow?

The technical side was interesting from the start, but the more important shift happened later. I realized the project would only become portfolio-grade if it **felt trustworthy**, not just clever.

## The problem was bigger than model output

A lot of AI projects look impressive in a demo, then fall apart as soon as someone asks normal product questions:

- What happens if the model fails?
- How does the user understand the result?
- Can they verify anything the system says?
- What happens to the uploaded file afterward?

Those questions changed how I thought about the product. Instead of treating the report as a wall of generated text, I started breaking the experience into more reviewable pieces:

- rubric scoring
- citation verification
- rewrite suggestions
- saved submission history
- a demo mode for quick evaluation

That structure made the interface easier to trust and also made the case study easier to explain.

## The most important work was in the framing

The strongest change was not a new model capability. It was deciding that DraftLens should present feedback in a way that felt grounded.

That meant:

- giving the user a clear workflow from upload to report
- separating feedback into visible categories instead of burying it in one response
- showing citation checks in a table so the output could be scanned quickly
- making privacy choices explicit, including deleting uploaded files after processing

Those decisions made the app feel less like "AI says things about your essay" and more like a product with a point of view.

## Why that matters for this portfolio

This portfolio refresh follows the same lesson. I do not want to show projects as isolated screenshots or generic cards. I want the site to explain:

1. what the product is for
2. what tradeoffs shaped it
3. why the implementation choices matter

That is why DraftLens now leads the site as a featured case study. It is the clearest example of the kind of work I want to keep doing: product-minded engineering with a strong frontend, honest constraints, and clear storytelling.
